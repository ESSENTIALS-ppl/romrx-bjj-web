The Case For (and Against) Static Stretching in BJJ

Static stretching is neither the villain nor the hero it is often made out to be. The research is clear on one thing: timing and duration decide whether it helps or hurts. Short static stretches or stretching after training are useful for BJJ. Long static holds right before you roll can temporarily sap your strength and power. Here is how to get the benefit without the downside.

What Is Static Stretching?

Static stretching means holding a muscle in a lengthened position for a set time — think holding a hamstring or hip stretch for 30–60 seconds. It is different from dynamic stretching, which uses movement (leg swings, hip circles) to warm the joint up.

The Case Against Static Stretching Before BJJ

For years, athletes were told to avoid pre-training static stretching, and there is real evidence behind it. Long static holds right before hard effort can reduce strength and power:

  • Stretches of 60 seconds or more per muscle cause meaningful strength drops of roughly 4–7.5% (Frontiers in Physiology, 2019).
  • A large review of 106 studies found holds of 60+ seconds cut muscle strength by about 7.5% on average (Kay & Blazevich, PubMed).
  • Major bodies like the NSCA and ACSM advise against heavy pre-event static stretching for strength and power sports (NSCA).

For a grip-and-power sport like BJJ, torching your strength right before a hard roll is a bad trade.

The Case For Static Stretching in BJJ

Here is the part most "never static stretch" advice misses: the harm is about long durations. Newer, higher-quality reviews show short static stretching is fine — and even helpful:

  • Stretches under 60 seconds per muscle have only trivial effects on strength (about 1–2%) (Frontiers in Physiology, 2019).
  • Holds under 45 seconds show essentially no performance downside (Kay & Blazevich, PubMed).
  • A 2024 meta-analysis of 83 studies found static stretching did not hurt jumping or sprinting, and even slightly improved jump performance (Journal of Sport and Health Science, 2024).
  • When part of a full warm-up, short static stretching may even lower injury risk in high-intensity movement.

And static stretching remains one of the most reliable ways to build long-term flexibility, which BJJ demands.

When Should BJJ Athletes Use Static Stretching?

Use this simple rule set:

SituationDo this
Right before rollingDynamic drills; keep any static hold under 60s per muscle
As a warm-up componentShort static holds (<45s) are fine inside a full warm-up
After classLonger holds (30–60s+) to build range — ideal time
Rest daysLonger holds and loaded stretches for flexibility gains

Key Takeaways

  • Static stretching is not bad — long pre-training holds are.
  • Holds of 60+ seconds before rolling can cut strength by 4–7.5%.
  • Holds under 45–60 seconds have little to no downside.
  • Save long static stretching for after class and rest days to build lasting range.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is static stretching bad for BJJ?

Only when done for long durations right before hard training. Short holds and post-training stretching are safe and useful.

How long can I static stretch before class?

Keep each muscle under about 45–60 seconds to avoid a strength drop, and pair it with dynamic movement.

Does static stretching prevent BJJ injuries?

As part of a full warm-up, short static stretching may lower injury risk in high-intensity activity, though evidence is mixed.

Static or dynamic stretching before rolling?

Lead with dynamic. Add only short static holds if a specific area needs it.
